About the artisans

Seven makers, five villages.

Hunza sits along the old trading route between Gilgit and Kashgar, and almost everything made here answers a practical question first. Wool became shawls because winters are long. Apricots were dried because the harvest arrives all at once and the road out was often closed. The decoration came later, and it stayed.

The people below are the ones who make what is on the shelf. They set their own prices. When you buy a shawl, the money goes to the person who wove it, minus what it costs to post the parcel.

A shawl takes three weeks. It should cost like it took three weeks.

How the crafts work

Wool. Fleece is washed in glacier melt, spun on a drop spindle through winter, then woven on a pit loom in narrow strips that are sewn together. Felt, or namda, skips the loom: the wool is soaked, rolled and worked by hand and foot until the fibres lock.

Wood. Walnut and cedar come from orchards and old houses in Altit and Ganish. Bowls are turned green and left to season for months. Carving copies the geometry on the fort doorframes, which is several centuries old and still being cut.

Stone. Ruby, lapis and jade are found in seams across the Karakoram. Most stones here are left rough, because polishing costs more than the stone is worth at this scale.

Orchard. Apricots go on flat stone roofs in July and stay there for a week. No sulphur, which is why they turn brown rather than orange. The kernels are cracked and pressed for oil.

The valley

Five villages along about forty kilometres of the Karakoram Highway.

The makers

Listed by village.

ZB

Zainab Bibi

Ganish · Wool

Makes: Patti Shawl, Namda Rug

Spins and weaves patti wool, and felts namda rugs. She learned from her mother and now teaches six women in the village, mostly in the months when there is no orchard work.

KS

Karim Shah

Altit · Walnut

Makes: Walnut Bowl

Turns bowls and platters on a foot-powered lathe his father built. He finishes everything with apricot kernel oil rather than varnish, so the pieces can be used for food.

DA

Didar Ali

Altit · Cedar

Makes: Cedar Panel

Carves panels and doorframes. He worked on the restoration of Altit Fort and copies its geometry into smaller pieces that fit in a suitcase.

SA

Sher Ali

Karimabad · Silver

Makes: Silver & Ruby Pendant

Draws his own silver wire and sets rough Karakoram stone. He has kept a shop on Baltit Road since 1998 and mostly sells to people walking up to the fort.

RB

Rukhsana Bano

Karimabad · Stone

Makes: Gemstone Cuff

Sets lapis and jade into cuffs and rings. She buys chips from the miners directly, which she says is the only way to know where a stone came from.

NK

Nasreen Karim

Gulmit · Embroidery

Makes: Hunza Cap

Stitches the silk edging on Hunza caps. A cap takes two evenings. She has made several thousand and can still name the year she started.

GA

Ghulam Abbas

Hyderabad · Orchard

Makes: Dried Apricots, Kernel Oil

Keeps forty apricot trees and dries the fruit on his roof. What does not dry well gets pressed for kernel oil by the women's co-op in Aliabad.
