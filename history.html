History of the craft

Trade, forts, wool and stone — how the shelf got here.

Hunza sits on a high corridor between South and Central Asia. For centuries caravans moved salt, silk, wool and metal along routes that later became the Karakoram Highway. Craft in the valley was never only decoration: it kept people warm, held food, and marked who belonged to which household.

What you see on this site is a student demonstration of those living skills — weaving, embroidery, silverwork and stone — still practised in villages from Aliabad to Gulmit.

  1. Before the road

    Household craft

    Wool was spun through winter and woven into patti cloth for coats and shawls. Caps, belts and bags were made at home. Wood from orchards became bowls and doors. Little left the valley except what traders could carry on foot or by mule.

  2. Forts & geometry

    Altit and Baltit

    The forts above the river taught generations of carvers the interlocking patterns that still run across doorframes and panels. Geometry was copied by eye, not from books — the same way Didar Ali works today.

  3. Silk Road echoes

    Stone and silver

    Lapis, ruby and other Karakoram stone moved with merchants. Local smiths learned to draw wire, set rough gems and oxidise silver so patterns stayed readable. Bangles, jhumkas and pendants became festival and bridal wear as much as trade goods.

  4. 1970s–90s

    The highway arrives

    When the Karakoram Highway opened the valley to buses and trekkers, workshops faced a new buyer: visitors who wanted something small enough for a suitcase. Caps, embroidered pieces and jewellery grew as souvenir crafts without replacing the older household work.

  5. Today

    Co-ops and open shelves

    Women's co-ops press oil, weave bags and sell together. Individual makers still price their own work. This demo store mirrors that idea: each piece is tied to a maker, a village and a method, even though nothing here is for real sale.
